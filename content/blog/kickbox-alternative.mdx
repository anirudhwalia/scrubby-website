If you are shopping for an email verification tool, Kickbox is a name you will run into fast. It is a solid, established service: clean API, real-time verification, a sensible scoring model. For the bulk of an average list (clearly valid mailboxes, obvious typos, dead domains) it does the job. So why do teams go looking for an alternative?

The answer almost always comes down to one category of address that standard verifiers cannot resolve: the catch-all.

## What every standard verifier does well

Before picking on the gap, it is worth being fair about what tools in this category get right. A competent verifier runs a predictable checklist:

- **Syntax** check, so `john@@domain.com` gets caught immediately.
- **Domain and MX** lookup, confirming the domain exists and accepts mail.
- **SMTP handshake**, opening a conversation with the receiving server to ask whether a specific mailbox exists.
- **Risk flags** for disposable domains, role-based prefixes, and known spam traps.

For a domain that answers honestly, this works. The server says "yes, that mailbox exists" or "no, it does not," and you get a clean verdict. Kickbox and most of its peers are reliable here.

## The catch-all wall

A catch-all (or accept-all) domain is configured to say "yes" to every address at the handshake stage, whether the mailbox exists or not. `realperson@company.com` and `asdfghjkl@company.com` both come back accepted. The SMTP conversation that normally produces a clean answer produces a meaningless one.

Faced with this, a standard verifier does the only honest thing it can: it gives up and labels the address "unknown," "accept-all," or "risky." That is not a bug, it is the ceiling of what an SMTP-only method can determine. The problem is what happens next on your side.

Catch-all configurations are common, especially among the B2B domains that matter most for outbound. On a typical prospecting list, the "unknown" bucket can swallow a large share of your contacts. You are then stuck with two bad options:

1. **Mail them anyway** and accept the bounces, the spam-trap risk, and the sender-reputation damage that follows.
2. **Cut them all** and throw away every real, reachable person hiding inside that bucket.

Either way you are guessing, and the verifier you paid for handed the decision back to you.

## What a catch-all-capable alternative does differently

This is the specific gap a tool like [Scrubby](https://scrubby.io) is built to close. Instead of stopping at the SMTP shrug, it applies deeper verification techniques to determine whether a mailbox behind a catch-all domain is actually live. The point is to convert that "unknown" pile into real verdicts: keep the addresses that resolve to a genuine inbox, drop the ones that do not.

When you are evaluating a Kickbox alternative, that is the feature to interrogate. Ask directly:

- Does it report a separate, actionable result for catch-all addresses, or does it just relabel them "risky" and move on?
- What share of a catch-all sample can it actually resolve to valid or invalid?
- Does it preserve deliverability by avoiding aggressive probing that can itself harm your sending domain?

A verifier that handles only the easy 70 percent of a list and waves at the hard 30 is not really finishing the job. The hard 30 is where bounces and spam complaints come from.

## A practical way to compare

You do not have to take anyone's word for it. Take a real sample list, ideally one heavy on B2B domains, and run it through both tools:

1. Note how many addresses each tool can verify as valid or invalid.
2. Count how many land in "unknown" or "accept-all" on each side.
3. For the addresses that one tool resolves and the other punts on, that delta is the practical value of switching.

The verifier that leaves you with the smallest "I have no idea" bucket is the one that saves you the most bounces and the most lost-but-real contacts.

## When email is not the only answer

One more thing worth saying plainly: even a perfectly verified list does not guarantee a reply. If a target account sits behind a catch-all and the deeper verification still cannot confirm a live mailbox, email may not be your strongest route to that account at all. Pairing a clean, validated list with a channel that does not depend on inbox placement, like the calendar-invite outreach approach behind [Kali](https://getkali.io), gives you a second path to accounts where the inbox is a dead end. Validation tells you which doors are real, and a second channel gives you another way through the ones that stay shut.

## The takeaway

Kickbox is a capable verifier, and for lists without many catch-all domains the differences may not matter much. But if your outbound leans B2B, the catch-all bucket is exactly where your money leaks: in the bounces you eat and the real prospects you delete out of caution. The right alternative is not the one with the flashiest dashboard. It is the one that turns "unknown" into a decision you can actually act on.
